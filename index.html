Mercury

  • Mercury is the smallest planet, just a bit bigger than our Moon, and it’s the closest planet to the Sun.
  • It has a rocky surface with many craters, similar to the Moon. No atmosphere means extreme temperature changes from day to night.
  • Very hot during the day (up to 430°C or 800°F) and freezing at night (-180°C or -290°F).
  • Mercury has no moons and takes just 88 Earth days to orbit the Sun.
  • Spins slowly, with one day lasting 59 Earth days.
35.02 km/s

Venus

  • Similar in size to Earth and known as Earth's "sister planet."
  • Thick clouds of sulfuric acid make Venus the hottest planet in the solar system. Its atmosphere traps heat in a runaway greenhouse effect.
  • The surface can reach up to 465°C (869°F), hotter than Mercury.
  • Venus rotates in the opposite direction to most planets, so the Sun rises in the west.
  • Takes 243 Earth days for one rotation, which is longer than its year.
29.78 km/s

Earth

  • Our home planet, third from the Sun, and the only one known to have life.
  • 71% of Earth’s surface is covered by water, and the rest is land, with mountains, valleys, deserts, and forests.
  • The atmosphere is 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, and trace gases, which support life.
  • Earth has one moon, which causes tides.
  • A day is 24 hours, and a year takes 365 days.
24.077 km/s

Mars

  • Known as the "Red Planet" because of its iron oxide-rich soil, Mars is about half the size of Earth.
  • Mars has two small, irregularly shaped moons, Phobos and Deimos.
  • Colder than Earth with an average temperature of about -60°C (-80°F). It has dust storms that can cover the whole planet.
  • Olympus Mons on Mars is the tallest volcano and the largest mountain in the solar system.
  • A Martian day is about 24.5 hours, close to Earth’s.
13.07 km/s

Jupiter

  • The largest planet, more than 11 times the diameter of Earth, with a massive storm system called the Great Red Spot.
  • Jupiter has over 79 moons, including the four largest: Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto, known as the Galilean moons.
  • Very cold at the top of its atmosphere but warmer as you go deeper.
  • Jupiter is a gas giant, meaning it doesn’t have a solid surface.
  • Spins very fast, completing one rotation in just 10 hours.
9.69 km/s

Saturn

  • The second-largest planet, known for its stunning ring system made of ice, rock, and dust particles.
  • Saturn has more than 80 moons, with Titan being the largest and having a thick atmosphere.
  • Very cold, with average temperatures of -178°C (-288°F).
  • The rings are made of countless particles, from tiny grains to large chunks.
  • Saturn is less dense than water and could float if you had a giant pool big enough.
6.81 km/s

Uranus

  • Uranus is an ice giant and has a unique tilt, rotating on its side like a rolling ball.
  • It has at least 27 moons, including Titania and Oberon.
  • Extremely cold, with an average temperature of -224°C (-371°F), making it the coldest planet in the solar system.
  • Uranus has faint rings made of ice and rock.
  • It has a strange magnetic field that is tilted at 59 degrees from its rotational axis.
5.43 km/s

Neptune

  • Similar in size to Uranus and also classified as an ice giant.
  • Known for its strong winds, which are the fastest in the solar system, reaching up to 2,100 km/h (1,300 mph).
  • Neptune has 14 moons, with Triton being the largest.
  • Appears blue due to methane gas in its atmosphere.
  • Neptune was discovered through mathematical predictions before it was seen through a telescope.
